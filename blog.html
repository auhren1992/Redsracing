What is Super Cup Racing? A Guide to Asphalt Oval Competition

If you're new to racing or curious about what RedsRacing competes in, here's your introduction to Super Cup asphalt oval racing — one of the most exciting forms of grassroots motorsport in the country.

Super Cup racing takes place on paved (asphalt) oval tracks, typically ranging from 1/4 mile to 1/2 mile in length. Unlike dirt track racing, asphalt ovals provide consistent grip levels and allow for higher speeds, making for intense bumper-to-bumper competition. The smooth surface rewards precise car setup, clean driving lines, and smart race strategy.

The cars themselves are purpose-built race machines based on production car platforms. They feature roll cages for safety, racing seats and harnesses, modified suspensions for oval-specific handling, and engines tuned for sustained high-RPM performance. Teams spend countless hours in the shop getting their cars dialed in for each track's unique characteristics.

A typical race night at a track like Grundy County Speedway includes practice sessions, qualifying (which determines starting positions), heat races, and a feature race. The feature is the main event — the one that counts most for points and bragging rights. Depending on the track, feature races can range from 25 to 100 laps of intense competition.

What makes Super Cup racing special is its accessibility. While professional motorsport series require millions of dollars in funding, Super Cup racing allows families and small teams like RedsRacing to compete at a high level. The series emphasizes close competition through rules designed to keep the playing field relatively level, ensuring that driver skill and team preparation matter more than budget size.

For fans, Super Cup racing offers affordable entertainment with non-stop action. The compact oval format means every seat in the grandstand provides a great view of the racing, and the close-quarters competition ensures there's always something exciting happening on track. It's the kind of racing where you can hear the engines roar, smell the rubber, and feel the excitement of each pass for position.

Whether you're a lifelong racing fan or attending your first race, Super Cup asphalt oval racing delivers an experience you won't forget. Come out to the track, support your local teams, and see why grassroots racing continues to thrive.

Behind the Scenes
March 2026

Inside the Shop: How RedsRacing Prepares for Race Season

With the 2026 season fast approaching, the RedsRacing shop has been a hive of activity. Every winter, the team tears down both race cars to inspect, repair, and upgrade every component. It's a labor-intensive process that can take weeks, but it's essential for ensuring reliability and performance when the season starts.

The process begins with a complete teardown. Every bolt gets inspected, every bearing gets checked, and every wear item gets evaluated. Suspension components are measured and rebuilt to specification. Brakes are inspected and fresh pads are installed. The engine gets a thorough inspection — compression tests, valve adjustments, and oil system checks are all part of the routine.

Adam, the team's technical lead, oversees the rebuild process with meticulous attention to detail. His four-plus years of experience working alongside Jon has given him an intimate understanding of what each car needs to perform at its best. "You can't cut corners in this part of the process," he explains. "The work we do in the shop during winter directly translates to what happens on race night."

Jon and Jonny are also hands-on in the shop. Both drivers believe that understanding every aspect of their cars makes them better behind the wheel. "When you know exactly how the suspension is set up, how the weight is distributed, and what the engine is doing, you can make smarter decisions during a race," Jon says.

Once the mechanical work is complete, the team turns its attention to cosmetics and safety equipment. Fresh paint, updated numbers, new decals, and a thorough check of all safety gear — harnesses, helmets, fire systems, and the roll cage — round out the preparation. By the time the season opener arrives, both the #8 and #88 cars will be ready to compete.
